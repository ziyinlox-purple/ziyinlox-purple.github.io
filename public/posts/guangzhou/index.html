Guangzhou: Delicious, Refreshing, and So Damp!

September 22, 2025 · 8 min · Theme PaperMod |  Suggest Changes
Table of Contents
  • 🐔🍜🍷 About the Food
  • 🎠 Chimelong Safari Park
  • 🚗 Skill Level Up

I never expected to find myself in Guangdong again. Without any prior planning, I’ve somehow ended up visiting Guangdong four times in the less than six months since I returned to China in April.

The first time was early April, staying in Shenzhen after coming from Hong Kong. The second was in May, visiting Shanwei with friends after returning from Dubai. The third was a layover at Baiyun Airport on my way back from Budapest via Guangzhou. Just a few days later, while “funemployed,” I helped a friend organize an event, which turned into a two-week tour of Guangzhou and Shenzhen.

This post focuses on my impressions of Guangzhou over those two weeks.


🐔🍜🍷 About the Food#

This trip gave me a fresh perspective on Guangzhou. The food being delicious is a given, but what really stuck with me was something a friend kept repeating while tipsy: “In Guangzhou, chicken tastes like chicken, and fish tastes like fish.”

Every city has its specialty: you eat duck in Nanjing, and you eat chicken in Guangzhou. As a chicken lover, Guangdong is paradise. The poultry here is truly on another level. While I haven’t made it to Qingyuan (the holy grail of chicken) yet, Guangzhou is already impressive. No matter what restaurant we visited, ordering White Cut Chicken was a mandatory ritual.

Personally, I love a “Q-bounce” (springy) texture—intestines and offal are my favorites! Aside from the chicken, the real joy on my tongue came from Cheung Fun (steamed vermicelli rolls) and Sheng Chang (pig’s fallopian tubes). If my time in Chiang Mai was an endless quest for the best Guay Tiao, my time in Guangzhou was an exploration of rice noodle rolls. My current favorite? The green bean, egg, and minced meat roll. The smooth, elastic roll meets the crunch of the beans—pure bliss! Also, the popularity of Sheng Chang in Guangzhou is incredible. When you chew on stir-fried Sheng Chang, it feels like it’s dancing on your tongue—crisp, tender, and perfectly seasoned.

I am a total Rice Noodle Roll head ;)

The Winner of this Trip: Tian Tian Wu (Sweet House). It fits my taste perfectly—unbeatable value without sacrificing flavor. Aside from the stir-fried Sheng Chang, I had the most tender White Cut Chicken of the entire trip there. It’s a local spot at the mouth of an alley. Rumor has it the boss lady didn’t speak a word of Mandarin until the influx of tourists forced her to learn. We had to wait for a table and ended up sharing a spot on the second floor with a couple. I realized I really love chicken paired with ginger-scallion oil. (Sorry, it was so good I forgot to take photos).

My Singaporean “bro” also shared his thoughts. He isn’t a picky eater at all, but even he has begun to appreciate the level of Cantonese chicken. He admitted it’s a total win over Singaporean Hainanese Chicken Rice. He also went out on his own to explore Wonton noodles and finally understood why I was so “picky” before. This reminds me of when he took me to a Michelin-starred Wonton noodle stall in Singapore. He expected high praise, but I just said, “It’s okay.” It was average—neither bad nor amazing. He later sent me a review from a famous Singaporean influencer who raved about that same spot. Sorry, I can’t lie—“it’s okay” was a generous rating, especially compared to the stall run by the auntie at the Chiang Mai University Social Sciences faculty.

I also didn’t expect that the most diverse range of cocktails I’d ever drink in a single night would be in Guangzhou. The day before leaving, I had dinner with friends I hadn’t seen in two years. Everyone had changed, and through their eyes, I saw my own growth—apparently, I look much more confident and extroverted now. Great!

We started with a light meal and a drink at Chui in the Rosewood Hotel. The vibe is nice; you can choose the terrace or stay inside for the live band. There was a Black band playing the night we went, and the lead singer was incredible. Chill atmosphere, but the drinks were just mediocre.

For the second round, we walked through the Guangzhou Tower area to the Wuyangcun neighborhood. The quality here was significantly higher. I had a crushed-ice drink—I think it was called “Crimson Desert”? My memory is a bit fuzzy.

We planned to go to Hope & Sesame (Miao Qian Bing Shi) for the third round but stumbled upon a quiet, charming spot on the way. We took a gamble on it. They specialize in tea-and-liquor fusions, and their Kombucha series was surprisingly good.

We were the only ones there; the decor was gorgeous.

The most ordinary-looking drink turned out to be the best.

Finally, we reached Hope & Sesame. Since it was less than an hour before closing, there was no line. It’s expensive, but undeniably good—many flavors were a pleasant surprise. By the end, my “legendary” friend was definitely buzzed, constantly muttering: “Chicken tastes like chicken, fish tastes like fish.” We calculated that the four of us finished 22 cocktails that night. A bit insane, but a very memorable experience.

We drank a lot. Honestly, the most surprising one was the unassuming ‘Black Box’.

On another night, we found a fantastic non-smoking bar, which I absolutely loved. We originally tried another spot, but the moment I stepped in, a wall of cigarette smoke hit me, and I bolted. On the way back, we saw a place that looked like a cafe from the outside. The door said “No Smoking,” it had seats available, and it was filled with girls—perfection! I sincerely hope more non-smoking bars pop up in China.

A responsible driver doesn’t drink. Yeah!

In Huadu, I also visited a coffee-by-day, bar-by-night spot called Knock Knock Log, located inside a residential compound.

A corner of the shop that looks like a tiny fishing village.

The “Manager” is a dog (I forgot the breed). He had just been neutered that day, so he wasn’t very happy lol. The dog was so distracting I don’t even remember if the drinks were good—they were fine, and the most important thing was that it was smoke-free.

This is the Manager after recovery, looking much happier lol (stolen from the owner’s WeChat).

I suspect the typos on their menu were intentional.

The biscuits were quite abstract—they looked and smelled like Play-Doh. The owner made them for fun and let us play with them, but my friend actually ate one and liked it. Fascinating Cantonese people lol.

This one is a bit abstract.

This one was modeled after the Manager 🍪; it tasted okay.

I strongly suspect that the combination of great food, late nights, and Guangzhou’s humidity caused me to break out severely. My skin really felt the “Chao”—a pun in Chinese meaning both “trendy/hip” and “damp.” It was so damp it felt like I was developing rheumatism.


🎠 Chimelong Safari Park#

When was the last time I went to a zoo? I can’t remember—maybe when I was 11. This time we drove to Chimelong. The five of us could have bought discounted tickets, but we missed the booking window because we stayed up too late. Oh well.

I only realized once I got there that Chimelong is privately owned. A friend told me that during the pandemic, the owner sold a piece of surrounding land just to afford to feed the animals. There’s construction for residential buildings everywhere now. It’s a bit poignant; definitely worth supporting.

The living conditions for the animals seemed quite good. Compared to other zoos, the management and environmental design allow the animals more freedom. Having recently visited Disney, I feel Chimelong could learn a thing or two from Disney or Universal in terms of merchandise design and IP collaborations to help fund even better care for the animals.

It was scorching hot, but the weather was beautiful.


🚗 Skill Level Up#

Most importantly: I finally mastered driving in Guangzhou!

I got my license back in 2020, but like many of my friends, I hadn’t touched a car since the exam. I’ve always wanted to practice, but I never had a strong enough reason—until my trip to Norway. If I could drive proficiently, I’d have more rental options abroad, making travel much more enjoyable. So, I asked my sister and aunt to accompany me for a couple of days of practice until I could manage the basics.

When planning the Guangzhou trip, a friend told me her dad’s car was available for us to drive to Shenzhen. The original plan was for my Singaporean and Taiwanese friends to swap their licenses for temporary ones because I wasn’t confident. However, on the first day, my trial run was great, and my friend praised me. That positive feedback was all I needed. I got hooked. I ended up being the designated driver for the entire trip—navigating daily meals, the crowded city center, and even the highway to Shenzhen.

It’s like what I mentioned in a previous post: when you aren’t a great swimmer, jump into the sea. When you get back to the 1.9m pool, you realize it’s nothing; you won’t drown. Surfing is the same—the more you fall, the faster you learn. Driving is no different. Even as a total “blank slate,” I handled night-time highway driving and the chaotic mix of electric bikes and complex traffic in Guangzhou. When problems arise, you face them. Prepare the basics, but remember that practice is the best teacher. (I’m not encouraging recklessness; I did do my homework and practice first!)

Now I’m looking forward to using my driving skills when I head to South America at the end of the year. Hehe.
