Argentina Travelogue (II): Stepping into Iguazu, Enveloped by Roaring Waters, Lush Greens, and Wild Creatures

This post mainly recounts the trip to Iguazu Falls.

🧳 Preparation#

The trip to Iguazu Falls was finalized two months before we even set foot in Argentina. It was the first part of our itinerary to be locked in, so we reaped the rewards of early planning—cheap flight tickets.

We bought tickets for Flybondi, only to discover afterward that “Red Flag” posts about them are all over social media. Apparently, this airline has a reputation for being quite chaotic. But what could we do? The tickets were already paid for and non-refundable, so we just kept a positive attitude and hoped for the best🤷.

As it turns out, airlines in Argentina—and South America in general—aren’t particularly reliable. This was just the beginning of our “stories” with various airlines. Over the two months following our purchase, Flybondi changed our flight time three times, eventually delaying the departure by eight hours. Fortunately, we hadn’t planned to do much on our arrival day anyway; otherwise, our short trip (one night and one full day) would have been severely compromised.

Transportation in Iguazu mostly consists of buses or driving. Our guesthouse offered a shuttle service, so we coordinated directly with their staff for pickups. If you prefer the bus, there are plenty of guides available online. I recall the round-trip price between the airport and our guesthouse for four people was 38,000 Pesos (if I remember correctly).


🗺️ Itinerary#

We saw some travelers online sharing that you can hire a private car to go to the Brazilian side of the falls and back on the same day without a visa. However, we also saw posts mentioning random spot checks. Since three out of the four of us hold Chinese passports and none of us had a Brazilian visa, we decided to play it safe. We chose to visit only the Argentine side of the falls and skipped the Brazilian side.

Our plan: Rest well after arriving at the guesthouse, head to the falls at 8:00 AM the next day, and catch a 3:30 PM flight back to Buenos Aires.

Tickets were purchased online via the official website, costing 35,000 Pesos per person for foreign tourists. A friend accidentally bought an extra ticket thinking identity info was required for each, but it turns out you just need the QR code. Besides standard tickets, you can also book various “tours” through travel sites depending on your needs.

Official ticketing website: https://ventaweb.apn.gob.ar/reserva/parques


🏞 Reaching the Falls#

The Argentine side of the falls is located within Iguazu National Park. Once inside, you can take a small train, which requires an additional fee. The train follows a schedule, stopping at a few stations along the way, with the final stop being the “Garganta del Diablo” (Devil’s Throat) station.

After getting off the train, it’s a 15-20 minute walk to reach the falls. The path crosses several rivers, and if you’re lucky, you’ll spot local wildlife, like the Coatimundi (commonly known as a Coati).

You’ll also see various butterflies, such as the “88 Butterfly.” While we were waiting to board the train to leave, one of these butterflies even landed on my bag.

Truly, the world’s widest waterfall is breathtaking. Looking down into the abyss, you feel as though you could be swallowed by the rushing torrents at any moment.

It’s worth noting: if you go to Iguazu, keep your food well-stored and do not feed the local animals. As we were leaving the falls, the Coatis seemed to have memorized the train schedule, knowing that passengers often have stray food. During the passenger changeover, they scurry onto the seats to scavenge and even get into fights with each other.

Coatis have a very keen sense of smell, which can lead to aggressive behavior (like scratching or biting tourists to grab food). While my friend and I were sitting on a wooden bench waiting for the train, a Coati walked right up to her bag and started sniffing—she did indeed have a pack of candy inside. My friend thought it was cute and didn’t move at first, but a local woman nearby quickly pulled her away, signaling us to stay clear of these animals.


🍚 About Dining#

I don’t remember much about what we ate in Iguazu because our stay was so brief. Due to the flight delay, we didn’t take off until night. Dinner was basically instant noodles cooked at the guesthouse. By the time we reached the airport and finally made it to Iguazu, it was nearly 2:00 AM before we got to sleep. We could hear the wind howling outside in the middle of the night and feared it would rain the next day, but it turned out to be perfectly sunny—what a relief!

Breakfast was eaten at the entrance of Iguazu National Park. There are cafes, bakeries, and snack shops near the gate. The food wasn’t great, but it was edible. There are also several souvenir shops and stalls selling local handicrafts nearby.

The only proper restaurant meal we had was after our tour. Since we didn’t do the “Great Adventure” boat ride into the falls, we just took the train to the main lookout and left, so we had plenty of time. If you have the energy, you can get off at the middle station and hike the Upper or Lower circuits.

We heard there was a great local fish restaurant nearby. Since we finished early, were starving, and knew the airport wouldn’t have many options, we paid our shuttle driver a little extra to take us to the restaurant and then to the airport. The restaurant is shown below.

We ordered a grilled fish and a risotto, among other things I’ve forgotten. Overall, it was quite good. The grilled fish featured a passion fruit sauce combined with other roasted vegetables. My only complaint was that the fish skin was a bit too charred and bitter. For four people, including the default 10% tip, it came to about 30,000+ Pesos per person.


💡 Important Notes#

We went in early-to-mid November. Personally, I didn’t encounter many mosquitoes, but another group of friends went the very next day and reported being swamped by them. I suspect it depends on exactly which trails you take.

If you are visiting during the local spring or summer, definitely bring mosquito repellent. The mosquitoes there are fierce. I got bitten once in Buenos Aires; I woke up in the middle of the night to one buzzing in my ear. I slapped it—leaving a massive bloodstain—and my hand swelled up for nearly a week.

Additionally, the path to the falls is an iron catwalk. Halfway through, we encountered a large foreign man who had tripped and gashed his forehead. He was sitting on the side with blood streaming down. Please be careful while walking!
